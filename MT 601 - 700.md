Thought for 57s

I dug into the DR references and the key pattern is this:

Stealth verbs are treated as player-facing actions with observer-dependent results, not as generic room emotes. The sources clearly support that HIDE is opposed by others’ Perception, SEARCH can reveal hidden characters, STALK follows a target while hidden, SNEAK is room-to-room hidden movement, and AMBUSH is a stealth contest that grants a hit bonus and often folds into the actual attack flow rather than being a separate visible “pre-attack” to everyone.

The other important takeaway is that DR documentation is good at describing who may notice and what the mechanic does, but it is not consistently public about exact per-perspective message strings for every verb. In other words, the behavior is documented more clearly than the literal text output. So the right move for DireMUD is to mirror the message model rather than trying to fake exact DR strings.

Here’s the design I’d lock in for Aedan.

For hide:

Actor should always get a direct result message such as “You slip into hiding,” “You struggle to conceal yourself,” or “You fail to find concealment.”
Room should get nothing by default.
Observer who beats the stealth contest strongly enough should get a reveal-style message such as “Jekar slips into hiding” or “You notice Jekar trying to hide.”
Target-specific output is not needed because hide is not target-directed.
This matches the DR behavior that other characters with enough Perception may notice you hide, and if they do not, they can later search for you.

For sneak:

Actor gets a success/failure movement message.
Room gets no normal movement broadcast if the sneak remains undetected.
Observers who partially detect should get vague output, not full identity, such as “You notice movement nearby.”
Observers who strongly detect should get identity plus movement, such as “Jekar slips north, trying to remain unnoticed.”
No target message because sneak is not target-directed.
That matches the DR model where SNEAK is hidden room-to-room movement and detection is observer-dependent rather than globally announced.

For stalk:

Actor gets a direct setup/persistence message like “You begin stalking training dummy” or “You continue stalking training dummy.”
Target player should only get a message if they win the relevant perception contest. If they do, they should get a direct message like “You sense someone following you” or “You spot Jekar shadowing you.”
Room should generally get nothing unless the stalk is blown.
NPC targets should not receive a player-visible message. Instead, their AI awareness state should change internally if they detect the stalk.
That matches DR’s hidden-follow behavior: STALK is meaningful because it is not automatically announced to the target, and detection should feel earned.

For search:

Actor gets explicit active-perception feedback: “You carefully search your surroundings.”
On success, actor gets “You spot Jekar!”
Hidden target player should get a direct target message on reveal, such as “Jekar notices you!” or “You’ve been spotted!”
Room should usually stay quiet unless you want a light generic emote like “Jekar searches the area carefully.”
NPC targets do not need a visible target message; it is enough to break stealth and adjust AI state.
This matches DR’s pattern that SEARCH is an active counter to stealth and that Perception is the main defense against hidden threats.

For observe:

Actor gets “You study your surroundings carefully.”
Room can get a low-key visible emote such as “Jekar pauses to study the area.”
No target message because it is broad awareness, not a direct action against one entity.
Any stealth detection should be surfaced to the observing actor, not broadcast broadly.
This is the cleanest DR-like extension of the documented Perception behavior even though the public docs are much thinner on exact OBSERVE messaging than on HIDE, SEARCH, and STALK.

For ambush:

Actor should get a strong explicit opener, for example “You ambush training dummy!”
Target player should absolutely get a target message if the ambush lands or is detected, such as “Jekar ambushes you!” That is important because ambush is a direct hostile action.
Room should get a room message on execution, not on setup, such as “Jekar bursts from hiding and ambushes training dummy.”
NPC targets do not need a user-facing “target message,” but they should internally react: set surprise, shift awareness to alert, possibly lose first action.
This matches DR’s documented ambush behavior as a stealth contest that grants a hit bonus and is tightly tied to the actual attack event.

So the decision I’d lock in is this:

Direct hostile or reveal actions should have actor + room + player-target messaging.
Non-hostile stealth setup actions should have actor messaging, observer-conditional messaging, and usually no room broadcast.
NPCs should not “see a text message.” They should react through AI state changes. Only players should receive target-specific text for being searched out, stalk-detected, or ambushed.

That gives you the right DR feel:

stealth is quiet unless noticed
detection is asymmetric
combat openers are loud and unambiguous
NPCs behave as if they noticed, instead of “reading” the same text players do

Here’s the deep-dive report before the next task batch.

DragonRealms’ modern locksmithing model centers on a single Locksmithing skill that replaced the older separate Lockpicking and Disarm Traps skills in DR 3.0. That unified skill is used to pick locks, disarm traps, and support related systems like lockpick carving and fixing, and its most common gameplay use is opening creature-dropped containers, usually called “boxes.”

The core gameplay loop is very close to the one you already outlined: players acquire a creature-dropped box, inspect or assess it, deal with any trap, pick the lock, then open and loot it. The public documentation and player script ecosystem both reflect that this is a multi-step workflow, not a single “open chest” action. Community scripts built around DR’s locksmithing explicitly automate repeated cycles of picking boxes, handling trap-related steps, looting contents, and then dismantling or disposing of the box, which strongly reinforces that DR expects locksmithing to be a sustained, repeatable gameplay loop.

The DISARM command in DR is richer than a simple binary “disarm trap.” It supports multiple modes including CAREFUL, NORMAL, QUICK, BLIND, ANALYZE, IDENTIFY, HARVEST, and ALL, which tells us two important things. First, trap work is not a single mechanical step; players can choose different risk/speed behaviors. Second, DR treats traps as objects with follow-on interaction after disarming, because ANALYZE and HARVEST exist as post-disarm interactions.

That post-disarm detail is especially important. DR explicitly allows players to harvest trap components after a trap has been disarmed, but only from the last trap disarmed on that box. If you move on to another trap, you lose access to analyze or harvest the earlier one. That means a DR-faithful system should not just track “trap removed”; it should also remember the most recent successfully disarmed trap as a separate state for possible follow-up interaction.

On the lock side, DR’s PICK behavior is strongly tool-driven. The current documentation says picking a lock requires a lockpick in hand or the presence of a worn lockpick ring, and the other hand must be empty or holding the locked object. That means DR locksmithing is not only skill-gated; it is also equipment-gated. A faithful implementation should eventually care about at least three things: whether the player has a valid pick, whether it is accessible, and whether their hand state is valid for the action.

The lockpick ring behavior is also more specific than “a container for picks.” The accessible pick is effectively the top / last-added pick, and it is the one used for picking or repair until removed. That implies DR locksmithing has a notion of active tool selection by stack order, not just inventory possession. You do not need to implement the full ring system immediately, but if you want the DR feel later, your tool model should allow “currently active pick” rather than treating all picks as identical abstract resources.

A key DR design principle is that information is imperfect. Public references consistently frame locksmithing around difficulty ladders, challenging traps, and practical training ranges, but not around exact numeric readouts to the player. Even the ecosystem around locksmithing ladders is community-generated and approximate, and players rely on “this lock feels easy / tricky / dangerous” style inference much more than explicit stat disclosure. For DireMUD, that means your inspect/appraise phase should produce approximate, descriptive output, not exact lock or trap difficulty numbers.

Another important DR behavior is that failure is not binary. The public docs do not expose precise formulas, but the command structure and related scripting culture make clear that players expect different operational modes, trap-trigger risk, and lockpick breakage risk. In practical terms, the DR loop behaves like: fail safely, make partial progress, succeed, or critically fail in a way that triggers a trap or destroys a tool. Even though some of those outcomes are more strongly documented in older Lockpicking / Disarm Traps pages than in the modern concise Locksmithing page, the merged Locksmithing model clearly inherits both traditions.

For message design, DR’s locksmithing interactions behave much more like non-hostile skill actions than like combat. The actor gets the full detailed feedback. The room generally gets light, flavor-level activity text such as someone studying, picking at, or working on a box. There is usually no target-player perspective, because the target is a container, not another player. The exception is when a trap triggers in a way that causes obvious room-visible effects. In your system, that means the correct rule is: actor gets detailed messages, room gets mild activity messages, and NPCs react through damage or awareness if a trap has world effects rather than “receiving” player-style text.

One especially valuable DR-inspired insight is that locksmithing is not only about success/failure; it is also about workflow friction. Players care about carrying boxes, managing picks, pausing when they are mind locked, and dealing with trash, gems, or harvested trap components. The fact that public player scripts automate steps like removing armor, cycling through boxes, pausing when experience is capped, and throwing away worthless results shows that DR locksmithing lives at the intersection of survival skill, inventory management, and loot economy. If you want the same feel, your first pass should focus on the core box/trap/lock loop, but your object model should leave room for later extensions like pick durability, harvested trap parts, and post-open loot handling.

So the most faithful design conclusions for DireMUD are these. First, keep boxes as explicit objects with lock state, trap state, and optional remembered “last disarmed trap” state. Second, make inspect or appraise descriptive rather than numeric. Third, treat disarm as a risky multi-outcome action that can also enable analyze/harvest later. Fourth, require an actual or at least modeled pick tool for pick. Fifth, message locksmithing as a mostly actor-facing survival interaction, with only light room text unless a trap goes off. And sixth, do not collapse the workflow into one command, because DR’s feel depends heavily on the fact that every stage is its own decision point.

My recommendation is to structure the next implementation in three passes. Pass one: box objects, inspect, disarm, pick, open, and simple trap effects. Pass two: lockpick items, active pick selection, and breakage. Pass three: analyze/harvest of the last disarmed trap plus richer loot handling. That ordering gets you the DR loop quickly without forcing the entire tool economy in one shot.
